Flow Matching For Generative Modeling

Preliminaries

The objective of generative modeling is to learn the underlying distribution pdata(x)p_{data}(x)pdata​(x) of the training data xxx. This is typically achieved by training a generative model pmodel(x)p_{model}(x)pmodel​(x) to approximate pdata(x)p_{data}(x)pdata​(x), allowing for the generation of new samples from the learned distribution.

Let XXX be a complete and separable metric space such as Ω⊆Rn\Omega \subseteq \mathbb R^nΩ⊆Rn. Then, the space of probability measures P(X)\mathcal{P}(X)P(X) is defined as the set of all Borel probability measures on XXX.

Generative models usually aims to learn a mapping from a simple distribution (e.g., Gaussian) to the complex data distribution pdata(x)p_{data}(x)pdata​(x) by transforming samples from the simple distribution into samples from the data distribution.

Let μ∈P(X)\mu \in \mathcal{P}(X)μ∈P(X) be the simple distribution, e.g. μ=N(0,I)\mu=\mathcal{N}(0, I)μ=N(0,I), and let ν∈P(X)\nu \in \mathcal{P}(X)ν∈P(X) be the data distribution. The goal is to learn a mapping f:X→Xf: X \to Xf:X→X such that f∗μ=νf_{*}\mu = \nuf∗​μ=ν, where f∗μf_{*}\muf∗​μ is the pushforward measure of μ\muμ under the mapping fff defined by f∗μ(A):=μ(f−1(A))f_{*}\mu(A) := \mu(f^{-1}(A))f∗​μ(A):=μ(f−1(A)), where A∈BXA\in \mathcal{B}_XA∈BX​ is a Borel set.

f∗:P(X)→P(X)μ↦f∗μ=ν\begin{align*} f_{*}: \mathcal{P}(X) &\to \mathcal{P}(X) \\ \mu &\mapsto f_{*}\mu = \nu \end{align*} f∗​:P(X)μ​→P(X)↦f∗​μ=ν​

But in reality, we only have the samples from the data distribution ν\nuν. So the best we can do is to approximate ν\nuν by

ν≈1N∑i=1Nδxi\nu \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \delta_{x_i} ν≈N1​i=1∑N​δxi​​

where xi∈Xx_i\in Xxi​∈X is the sample data and δxi\delta_{x_i}δxi​​ is the Dirac measure centered at xix_ixi​.

This can be viewed as a semi-discrete optimal transport problem, where we aim to find a mapping f:X→Xf: X \to Xf:X→X that pushes forward the simple distribution μ\muμ to the empirical distribution ν\nuν.

Data Manifold Hypothesis

We view the real distribution as a distribution over M\mathcal{M}M, where M\mathcal{M}M is a manifold embedded in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn and nnn is the dimensionality of the data. More precisely, we assume that the data distribution ν\nuν has support on a lower-dimensional manifold M⊂Rn\mathcal{M} \subset \mathbb{R}^nM⊂Rn, typically with dim⁡(M)≪n\dim(\mathcal{M}) \ll ndim(M)≪n. This is the data manifold hypothesis, which posits that high-dimensional data often lies on or near a lower-dimensional manifold.

Theoretical Framework vs. Practical Implementation

Theoretical assumption:

The true data distribution pdata(x)p_{data}(x)pdata​(x) is absolutely continuous with respect to the volume measure on the data manifold M\mathcal{M}M, i.e., there exists a smooth density function ρdata:M→R+\rho_{data}: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}_+ρdata​:M→R+​ such that:

dν=ρdata(x) dvolM(x)d\nu = \rho_{data}(x) \, d\text{vol}_\mathcal{M}(x) dν=ρdata​(x)dvolM​(x)

Practical reality:

We only observe finite samples {xi}i=1N\{x_i\}_{i=1}^N{xi​}i=1N​ from the true distribution, leading to the empirical distribution:

ν^N=1N∑i=1Nδxi\hat{\nu}_N = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \delta_{x_i} ν^N​=N1​i=1∑N​δxi​​

Note that each Dirac measure δxi\delta_{x_i}δxi​​ is not absolutely continuous with respect to the volume measure, since {xi}\{x_i\}{xi​} has zero volume but δxi({xi})=1\delta_{x_i}(\{x_i\}) = 1δxi​​({xi​})=1.

Regularization assumption: We assume that the empirical distribution ν^N\hat{\nu}_Nν^N​ converges weakly to the true continuous distribution ν\nuν as N→∞N \to \inftyN→∞:

ν^N⇀νas N→∞\hat{\nu}_N \rightharpoonup \nu \quad \text{as } N \to \infty ν^N​⇀νas N→∞
